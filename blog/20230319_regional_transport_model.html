# TransLink and transport modelling for the Greater Vancouver Area

Tranportation modelling for the Greater Vancouver area is done by TransLink (South Coast British Columbia Transportation Authority). TransLink is very intent on improving its modelling practice and have had a Transport Model Enhancement Program for a long time. They are also very transparent about it and document everything. It is a wonderful resource to learn from!

# The model

The Greater Vancouver model is named the Regional Transport Model (RTM) and its current version is RTM 3.6. The model is a super-charged four-steps models which includes steps to model shared vehicles, park and ride, time of day departure, pedestrian and cycling. TransLink is now looking to develop an Activity Based Model to be able to model more accurately post-pandemic mobility behaviour changes, increased focus on equity analysis and the bus-based transit expansion strategy.

Every year, TransLink updates the road and the transit network to better reflect actual supply. Every five to six years they run a trip diary survey and screenline surveys. They also benefit from fresh census data every 5 years.

Modelled travel times and volumes through network links are validated against a mixture of observed data coming from Google API, purchased data (INRO / Tomtom / StreetLight), bluetooth collected data and floating car survey.

# Modelling community and model governance

TransLink organizes and animates the transport modelling community as part of their Transport Model Enhancement Program. Among the activities they organize:

  • They run yearly trainings to train the 30+ agencies in the Greater Vancouver area on the RTM.
  • They host yearly workshops with stakeholders to communicate model updates, including limitations.
  • They gather stakeholders feedback regarding model feature requirements (additional modelling capabilities, analysis capability).
  • They gather network improvements and fixes made by the agencies and integrate them back into the model for every other agency to benefit from it.

A bit of OSINT: looking through LinkedIn I found 4 modellers, and I suspect we would find more analysts.

Skimming through some of the reports, it is clear that TransLink, in addition to have a great modelling team, partners with consulting agencies and software vendors to develop their next generation of models.

Main local universities in Vancouver are University of British Columbia and Simon Fraser University. I did find a single class about transport modelling (opens new window), and no indication of a advanced research projects. So I expect that their is not a strong research community in the field of transport modelling, which in turns means it is hard to find trained profesionnals. It corroborates a discussion I had back in 2018 with a hiring manager from TransLink. BUT, I may have been looking in the wrong place!

# History of the model

In September 2022, Harvey Harrison made a presentation (opens new window) about the history of RTM. This is an awesome document to understand where the model coming and where is it going to, what was prioritized etc. It also shows that the current model is the result of decades of intent improvements.

The use of a vendor software (EMME) is credited as the stepping stone in the strategy of TransLink to federate all agencies under one model. Harvey highlights how the first decade was about improving base components (volume delay functions, park and ride, cost representation), how the model then evolved with ever increasing number of traffic analysis zones, representation of tolls and High Occupancy Vehicles to answer specific studies, how the 2010's saw an increased focus on representing the demand (representation of the value of time, time of travel, auto ownership, incomes, car sharing, non auto mode) to answer a wider range of questions non focused on peak hour commuting auto and transit trips.

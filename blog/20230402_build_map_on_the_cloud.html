# QField - mapping on the go

Long story short, my girlfriend recently started training to be an arborist. In addition to the physical requirements the job has, she also needed to learn quite a bit about trees: recognizing their species (in summer and in winter), their injuries, how to care for them etc. We live in Montreal, and this website (opens new window) proved very useful for learning how to identify tree speecies: it shows a map with every tree publicly manager on the island of Montreal, and their species. We would go on long walks to try and identify tree.

No surprise there: we are in Canada and maple tree is number one!

After a while, it became clear that some tree had outdated information, either the tree had been cut down, or new ones were missing, or the reported specy was incorrect. But if you look at the Quebio website you will see that there is no way to propose a modification or a correction to the database, and we became quickly frustrated with it.

We wanted to create our own tree database for Montreal, one we can modify as we walk around. I was already familiar with QGIS (opens new window) a Desktop-based Geographic Information System. The neat part is that it is free! But it works on computer, not on smartphone and we did not want to bring a laptop with us on our walks (mid-winter in Montreal goes down to -30C: no way we would stop and wait for the computer to boot up!). After investigating a little bit, I found QField (opens new window).

QField is a phone application which provides a very simple GUI to a Geographic Information System stored in the cloud (QField Cloud). The tutorial (opens new window) is rather well done, so I will just summarize it here:

  • create a QFieldCloud account
  • use QGIS to create a GIS project, create the various features and layers you need and style them
  • publish your project to QFieldCloud
  • install QField on your phone and connect to your account
  • you can start editing your GIS data on the go, be remember to synchronize your data from your phone to the cloud!
  • bonus point, it versions data changes allowing us to go back in time

All things considered, it tooks us a day (6-8 hours) to figure out how this all works, get familiar with it and start using it. Our project is public and stored here (opens new window).

# Alternatives and pricing

We started using QField as it was still in beta access, everything was free, space unlimited... and we abused it. Now that QField is general access, there is a limit of 100Mb for each project. Once can subscribe to get more (we currently have over 3Gb of geolocated photographs of tree!). And commercial applications must subscribe as well. We don't need it anymore but these are details which are important to consider and must be balanced with other more traditional offering:

  • ESRI (900 a year)
  • Mapbox (free until a certain usage, than steep increase with usage, need to write your own "Data edit applciation")
  • QGIS Cloud (free tier, then 65 euros/months + possible to pay for more memory and functionality)

# More than trees

In the past I have seen cities struggling with data management: their data is often out-dated and costly to maintain up to date. Most of them don't have "on-the-field" means of collecting data so everything is done from the office: take photos from the field, or Google Earth/Google StreetView and try to extract info from there. It looks like QField could be a cheap and quick way for them to field test having data gathering tools on the field. Maybe the results shows that it does not increase data quality or reduce cost. But if, in the contrary, it does improve data quality and reduce cost, then it would be a nice proof-of-concept to push for a dedicated set of tools.

Among data which could be interesting to gather for a city:

  • traffic signals and curbside information
  • "to-dos" constructions and patching
  • network positions
  • trees
  • etc

# Fight "Social media bubbles" and echo-chambers in science

I was recently reminded why you need to do your own research when it comes to science.

During a meetup in 2018, a friend of mine was telling me how his company had been partnering up for the past year with a software vendor to fund research and develop state-of-the-art solutions for their industry. He has a Ph.D. and was very excited to have the opportunity to both advance his company goals and the world of research. All his colleagues were pumped, the local collaborators were pumped: all greenlights! After hearing more details, the research he was describing started to sound familiar, so I pointed out a couple of research papers I thought would be useful. It turned out that the software vendor had recycled 40-years-old research papers and sold that as innovation to my friend. He thought he was keeping up-to-date by attending local conferences, discussing with colleagues and alumni from his university, but unfortunately "social media bubble" is also a thing when it comes to science. His direct social network acted as an echo-chamber and amplified his initial belief.

How can we counteract echo-chambers? I don't have the perfect solutions but here are a few leads to get you thinking:

  • Surround yourself with people smarter and more knowledgable than you.
  • Do your own research, learn about the history of your field.
  • Attend events and conferences outside of your usual circle.
  • Seek out independant expert opinion.
